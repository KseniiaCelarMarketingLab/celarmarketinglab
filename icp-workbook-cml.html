Step 1 / 8 · Foundation

Your business, right now

Before defining who you're chasing, ground this in who you already serve. An ICP isn't invented — it's found in the pattern of people who already buy from you.

What does "ICP" actually mean? ICP stands for Ideal Customer Profile — a clear, specific description of the type of customer who gets the most value from what you offer, and who is the easiest and most profitable for you to serve. It's not a wish list of dream clients; it's a working definition built from real patterns in your own customer base.
1. In one sentence, what do you sell and to whom?
Plain language — no jargon, as if explaining it to a stranger at a dinner party
2. List your current top 3–5 customers or customer types
No analysis yet — just names or short descriptions. You'll look for the pattern in the next step.
Step 2 / 8 · Foundation

Your best customer

Pick one real customer from your list above — not your biggest, your best fit. Answer in their words, not yours.

Why not just "age, industry, company size"? Demographics tell you who someone is. They don't tell you why someone buys. Two companies can look identical on paper — same size, same industry — and one of them will never become your customer. This step is built around behavior: the real moment, frustration or event that pushed a real person to act.
1. What was happening in their business right before they reached out to you?
e.g. new hire, lost a client, launch coming up, leadership pressure
2. What specific moment, deadline or frustration made them act now — instead of putting it off another month?
What's a "trigger"? A trigger is not the underlying problem — it's the specific moment that pushes someone from thinking about something to actually acting on it. A business can have a marketing problem for years. The trigger is what makes them call you this Tuesday instead of next year: a lost deal, a new hire, a deadline, a bad experience with someone else.
3. What had they already tried before they found you?
Another agency, a freelancer, doing it themselves, nothing at all
4. In their own words — how do they describe the problem?
Why does exact wording matter? The words a customer uses themselves are usually more persuasive than the words a marketer would choose. If they say "I'm tired of posting into silence," that phrase belongs in your ads and your website — not a polished rewrite of it. Marketers call this voice of customer.
Write it exactly as they'd say it out loud, not how you'd phrase it for a brief
5. If they'd done nothing for another 6 months — what would that have cost them?
Lost revenue, lost time, a competitor pulling ahead, low morale — be specific if you can
Step 3 / 8 · Filters & triggers

The hard filters

Behavior explains why someone buys. These structural traits explain who can realistically buy at all.

What's "firmographics"? It's the company-level equivalent of demographics: size, industry, budget, location, structure. On their own these traits don't explain motivation — but they're the fastest way to filter out obvious mismatches before you spend time on anyone. A company with the right pain but no budget or authority to act still isn't a fit today.
Typical company size — employees, revenue, or both
Industries or business types that keep showing up
Realistic budget range they need to have to work with you
Not your price list — the minimum a prospect needs to be able to invest for this to make sense for both sides
Where are they located — which countries or regions do you actually want to serve?
What language(s) do they operate in day to day?

Step 5 / 8 · Objections & exclusions

Objections & decision process

The reasons they hesitate, and who actually has to say yes.

Objections & hesitations
What's an "objection"? An objection is the reason someone hesitates or says no — separate from the trigger that brought them to you. Someone can genuinely need what you offer and still stall on price, trust, or timing. Preparing a real answer in advance, instead of improvising in the moment, is what this section is for.
Select the ones you actually hear — then write your best response to each
Who makes the final call — the person you talk to, or someone else?
Why ask this separately? In many small and mid-sized EU businesses, the person you speak with isn't always the one who signs off — a partner, a board, or a co-founder often has to agree first. Marketers call this the difference between the user and the economic buyer. If you don't know who the buyer is, you can't prepare the right proof for them.
What do they need to see or hear before they say yes?
Typically, how long from first contact to decision?
Step 6 / 8 · Objections & exclusions

Who's not your customer

Defining who to say no to is as valuable as defining who to say yes to.

What's an "anti-ICP"? It's the mirror image of your ideal customer — the type of lead that looks promising on paper but consistently turns into a slow, exhausting, or unprofitable project. Every hour spent on a bad-fit client is an hour not spent finding or serving a good one, and a bad case study can quietly attract more of the wrong leads through referrals and your own portfolio.
Describe a lead that looked promising but turned into a bad-fit client
Common red flags — select what you recognize
What does it actually cost you when you take on a bad-fit client?
Time, team morale, cash flow, the quality of referrals you get afterward
Step 7 / 8 · Message

How to talk to them

Translate everything you've mapped so far into concrete words, channels and tone.

What's a "value proposition"? It's a one-sentence answer to "why should this exact customer choose you, in their own terms" — built from the trigger, the words and the transformation you already described, not a generic list of features.
In one sentence, what's the transformation you deliver for this exact customer?
Before → after. Use their language from Step 2, not marketing-speak.
Where do they spend time professionally?
These are your channels — the specific places worth showing up consistently, instead of spreading effort thin across everywhere at once.
What tone resonates with them?
What's the one myth or misconception you need to break before they'll trust you?
