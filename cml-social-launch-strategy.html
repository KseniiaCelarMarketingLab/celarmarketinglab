PHASE 01 / 06

Audience

Strategy starts with people, not platforms. Before you decide what to post or where — you need to understand who you're actually talking to, with enough precision to write a post they'd stop scrolling for.

WHY AUDIENCE COMES FIRST

Most founders build backwards: pick a platform, pick a format, then wonder why the content doesn't connect. Platform is distribution. Audience is the strategy.

A post that speaks directly to one person's specific frustration will always outperform a polished post aimed at everyone. If you can describe your audience in a sentence, you haven't gone deep enough yet.

The real question isn't "who is my audience?" — it's "what does my audience complain about at 11pm when nobody's listening?"
EU MARKET NOTE

EU audiences — especially NL, FR and DE — are more skeptical of marketing claims and respond better to specificity and concrete social proof than to aspirational language. "Results-driven" means little. A named client, a specific number, a verifiable outcome earns trust.

Who is your audience?
Age, role, situation — the more specific, the better
Where do they already spend time online?
Not where you want them — where they actually are
What keeps them up at night?
The frustrations they'd admit to no one
What do they deeply want?
The aspirations behind their day-to-day frustrations
PHASE 02 / 06

Positioning

Thousands of accounts exist in any niche. Posting more won't make you stand out. Positioning will — it's the specific, ownable place you occupy in your audience's mind.

DESCRIBING VS. POSITIONING

Most founders describe what they do. Positioned brands own a specific point of view that no one else is staking out.

DESCRIBING
"I'm a marketing consultant helping small businesses grow"
POSITIONING
"I build brand systems for EU e-commerce founders who are tired of competing on price"
A strong position attracts the right people AND repels the wrong ones. Both are valuable.
EU MARKET NOTE

If you serve multiple language markets, keep one core positioning — but let the voice flex per market. A direct, witty tone that lands in Amsterdam can feel too informal in Paris. The strategic position stays constant; the way you express it adapts.

Business / brand name
Industry / niche
Brand promise — one sentence
Who you help + what they get + why it's different. If it takes two sentences, it's not ready.
3 things only you can claim
Claims a competitor literally cannot make — your method, story, clients, or data
What is your audience doing instead?
Who / what are you really competing with?
PHASE 03 / 06

Message

Even a clear position can be expressed in words that make people scroll past. Your message is how your positioning sounds in real language — the words that make the right person think "that's exactly for me."

THE MESSAGE STACK

Think of your message in three layers: the hook (stops the scroll), the core message (what they remember), and the voice (what makes every post sound like you).

HOOK
A specific claim, counter-intuitive idea, or shared frustration that stops scrolling
CORE MESSAGE
The one idea they take away — every time, from every post
VOICE
The consistent personality that makes your content recognizable without your name
OBJECTION BRIDGE
The real reason they haven't taken the step yet — addressed through content
Test: read 10 of your posts with the name removed. Can someone identify they're from you? That's the voice test.
EU MARKET NOTE

American-style urgency copy ("DM me NOW!") underperforms in EU markets. Dutch audiences prefer direct honesty without hype. French audiences respond to sophistication and nuance. Build your message without manufactured pressure — add concrete social proof instead of urgency triggers.

Core message — the one idea behind everything you post
Not your tagline. The strategic belief you want to own in your niche.
Brand voice — how you sound
3 adjectives, then: "sounds like / doesn't sound like"
What objections does your audience have?
Why haven't they worked with someone like you yet?
Your hook formula — what stops the scroll?
A counter-intuitive idea, a specific number, a direct challenge. What makes someone pause on your content?
PHASE 04 / 06

Channel

Not all platforms are equal — and you don't have time to be everywhere. The right channel is where your audience already lives, with a format that matches your content strengths.

CHANNEL IS A STRATEGIC DECISION

The most common mistake: choosing a platform because you use it personally, or because a competitor is there. Channel choice should follow your audience research from Phase 1 — not your preferences.

INSTAGRAM
Visual B2C, lifestyle, products, emotional stories, 20–42
LINKEDIN
B2B, professional services, SaaS, thought leadership, 28–55
TIKTOK
Gen Z + millennial, entertainment-first, viral reach, fast iteration
YOUTUBE
SEO longevity, deep trust via long-form, all ages who research before buying
Rule: own 1–2 platforms completely before expanding. Mediocre presence everywhere is worse than excellent presence somewhere.
EU MARKET NOTE

LinkedIn over-indexes for B2B across Benelux and DACH. Instagram leads B2C in NL, FR and BE. Facebook remains relevant for 35+ in FR/DE community groups. GDPR consent rules limit ad-pixel tracking — organic saves, shares and DMs are your most reliable signal here, more so than paid reach metrics.

PHASE 05 / 06

Content Pillars

Pillars are your editorial categories — the recurring themes that give your audience a reason to keep following you. Without them, every post is a decision from scratch. With them, you have a system.

THE 70 / 20 / 10 BALANCE

Social is a relationship channel, not a broadcast channel. A brand that only sells is like someone who walks into a party and immediately pitches everyone in the room.

70%
EDUCATE + ENTERTAIN
20%
COMMUNITY + ENGAGE
10%
SELL + CONVERT
The goal is not followers. The goal is an audience that trusts you enough to buy — when the time is right for them.
EU MARKET NOTE

Don't translate pillar examples across language markets. A "Trust" post citing a Dutch case study won't resonate the same way in France — the case study itself needs to be local. Keep the pillar structure consistent; localize the content examples inside it per market.

PILLAR 1 — EDUCATE
Share knowledge that solves real problems
PILLAR 2 — BUILD TRUST
Prove you know your stuff — without stating it
PILLAR 3 — ENTERTAIN
Make them feel something — connection, curiosity, recognition
PILLAR 4 — CONVERT
Invite — without pressure
Content formats you'll use
Short video / Reels Carousels Single images Stories Long-form posts Podcasts / Audio Live sessions Polls / Q&As Newsletters
Recurring content series
A named, recurring format builds habit. Your audience knows when to expect it.
PHASE 06 / 06

Launch Calendar

The difference between a launch and "just posting" is intentional sequencing. The first 30 days set the algorithm, establish your tone, and turn strangers into returning readers. Don't wing it.

THE 4-WEEK LAUNCH SEQUENCE

Every launch benefits from a warm-up before the public announcement. Tell your network before you tell strangers. Get 10–20 people to engage on Day 1 — the algorithm rewards early momentum.

WEEK -1 (PRE-LAUNCH)
First 9 posts ready, profile optimized, 20 contacts briefed
WEEK 1 (LAUNCH)
Post hero content, supporters engage within first hour
WEEK 2 (BUILD)
Post daily, respond to every comment, follow 10 relevant accounts/day
WEEKS 3–4 (FIND RHYTHM)
Drop to sustainable cadence, test formats, cut what isn't working
The 90-day rule: accounts that become destinations posted consistently for 90 days before they "got lucky." Don't audit results before week 8.
EU MARKET NOTE

Local hashtags (#amsterdamondernemer, #marketingfr) reach a tighter, more relevant audience than broad global tags. Schedule in CET/CEST, not your tool's default UTC — a 1-hour drift quietly moves every post out of the commute window. Metricool and Later keep data EU-based, useful context if clients ask about GDPR-compliant tooling.
